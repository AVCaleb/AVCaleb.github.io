BIBLES

  1. The New American Standard Bible
    • Based on the American Standard Version (1901) which has been highly regarded for its scholarship and accuracy.
    • The translation is in a fluent and readable style according to current English usage.
  2. The Holy Scriptures (A New Translation from the Original Languages) by J. N. Darby
    • A more awkward English but very faithful to the original.
    • Recommended for further study into the original meanings.
    • Highly recommended by Watchman Nee.
  3. Young’s Literal Translation of the Bible by Robert Young
    • Translation by the author of Young’s Concordance.
    • A combination of this and Darby’s translation gives a good sense of the meaning of the original texts.
  4. The Scofield Reference Bible
    • One of the most popular and widely used reference Bibles in existence.
    • The revised edition eliminates some of the misleading notes about dispensationalism.
    • "This will be, I think, recognized everywhere as the most helpful reference Bible yet to be published in the English language." — Wilbur M. Smith
    • Note: Professor Wilbur M. Smith was probably the greatest evangelical bibliographer of his generation.
  5. The Emphasized Bible by Joseph Bryant Rotherham
    • A literal translation of the Greek and Hebrew texts with particular stress upon the grammatical emphasis of each verse by use of diacritical marks.
    • Recommended by Watchman Nee.
  6. The Interlinear Greek-English New Testament by Alfred Marshall
    • Available with NIV, NASB, or RSV texts.
  7. The Interlinear Bible by Jay Green
    • Four volumes.

CONCORDANCES

  1. The Analytical Concordance to the Bible by Robert Young
    • Contains 311,000 separate references to words and phrases in the Old and New Testaments (approximately 118,000 more than in any other concordance).

  3. Cruden’s Concordance by Alexander Cruden

ENCYCLOPEDIAS

  1. Dictionary of the Bible by William Smith (1813-1893)
    • Four volumes of approximately 900 pages each.
    • This dictionary consists of entries prepared by 60 of the outstanding Biblical scholars such as: Dean Alford, Bishop Ellicott, F. W. Farrar, J. B. Lightfoot, the Perownes, Samuel Tregelles, Canon Wordsworth, Bishop Westcott, etc.
    • Possesses lasting significance in spite of its age and obvious historical and archaeological limitations.
    • "Many articles unquestionably superior to similar articles contained in modern dictionaries." — Cyril J. Barber
  2. The Cyclopedia of Biblical, Theological and Ecclesiastical Literature by John McClintock and James Strong
    • One of the most exhaustive encyclopedias ever to be published in the English language.
    • Covers virtually every field of religious knowledge in its 31,000 articles.
    • The greatest contribution of this encyclopedia is its bibliographies.
    • Republished by Baker, Grand Rapids 1968-1970, 12 volumes.
  3. The Dictionary of Christ and the Gospels by James Hastings
    • Two volumes, covers virtually every facet of study relating to Christ in the Bible and world literature.
    • The most valuable of all the dictionaries edited by Dr. Hastings.
    • "This is a set that ought to be indexed page by page, for the reader will come upon one surprise after another in the titles of articles written by some of the best scholars of the western world at the beginning of our century." — Wilbur M. Smith
  4. The International Standard Bible Encyclopedia by James Orr
    • The most comprehensive evangelical Bible dictionary in the English language.
  5. Unger’s Bible Dictionary by M.F. Unger
    • One of the best single volume Bible dictionaries.
    • Based on the Hasting’s dictionary of the Bible.
    • Contains new material, especially archaeological updates.
  6. Hasting’s Dictionary of the Bible by James Hastings
    • Four volumes.
    • Too technical for the layman.

USEFUL ONES:

  1. Lange’s Commentary Series by John Peter Lange, edited by Dr. Philip Schaff
    • Old Testament 8 volumes, New Testament 10 volumes.
    • One of the best multivolume commentaries available.
    • An outstanding conservative theologian and New Testament scholar in the last century.
    • Charles Haddon Spurgeon praised it, and several generations of Bible students have used it.
    • “We have nothing equal to them as a series” — C.H. Spurgeon
  2. Biblical Commentary on the Old Testament by C. F. Keil and Franz Delitzsch
    • 10 volumes, combines sound exegesis with sound exposition.
    • Best among the Old Testament commentaries, esp. Genesis, Samuel, Kings, Chronicles, Isaiah and Minor Prophets.
    • “There is nothing to compare with Keil and Delitzsch” — Wilbur Smith
  3. The Greek Testaments by Dean Henry Alford
    • 4 volumes.
    • All critics speak of Alford with respect.
    • Strongly recommended by Watchman Nee.
    • “He is for the present at any rate, indispensable to the student of the original” — C.H.Spurgeon
  4. The Expositor’s Greek New Testament by Eerdman’s
    • 5 volumes.
    • Highly recommended by prof. Wilbur Smith.
  5. Commentaries on the New Testament by R.C.H. Lenski
    • 12 volumes.
    • Highly recommended by Prof. Wilbur M. Smith: “The greatest series of commentaries on New Testament written in this century by an American scholar”.
    • “Gospel of John” is his best.
    • “I profit greatly from his sane and spiritually sensitive exposition” — Warren W. Wiersbe
  6. Gnomon of the New Testament by John Albert Bengel
    • Bengel was a great German Bible scholar.
    • A classic.
    • “Gnomon” means “a pointer”; the author wanted his books to point the way, not to explain Scripture exhaustively.
    • “It is the Scholar’s delight! Bengel condensed more matter into a line than can be extracted from pages of other writers.” — C.H. Spurgeon
    • “He so often throws open a single turn the secret chambers of the Word” — Marvin Vincent
    • “A marvel of condensation and spiritual insight; must always remain a classic.” — Philip Schaff
  7. The New Bible Commentary by Inter-Varsity Christian Fellowship of London (1952)
    • Probably the most dependable, scholarly, stimulating and comprehensive one-volume commentary on the Bible in English language.
  8. New International Commentary on the New Testament
    • “No one can afford to be without it.” — Warren W. Wiersbe
    • The volumes, say, of Leon Morris on John and of John Murray on Romans are regarded as masterpieces.
  9. The Expositor’s Bible Edited by William Robertson Nicoll
    • 6 volumes.
    • This set contains expositions by both conservative and liberal scholars (so, not everything in the original set is worth owning).
    • The recommended volumes are:
      • Dod (Genesis)
      • Chadwick (Exodus and Mark)
      • Kellogg (Leviticus)
      • Blaikie (Joshua, I and II Samuel)
      • Adeney (Ezra, Nehemiah and Esther)
      • Maclaren (Psalms and Colossians)
      • Moule (Romans)
      • Findlay (Galatians and Ephesians)
      • Plummer (Pastoral Epistles and the Epistles of James and Jude)
      • Lumby (Epistles of Peter)
      • Milligan (Revelation)
  10. Exposition of the Bible by John Gill
    • A deeply devotional, highly allegorical exposition.

Note on the “copy books” for Bible Study

Bible study is like learning to write - the process begins by copying the writing of others, then you develop your own style. For each book of the Bible there are “copy books” that should be used as guides during study.

    • This work preceded Manton’s treatment. Manton regarded this exposition with such awe that he purposely avoided duplicating any of its material in his own work.
  2. Commentary on Jude by Thomas Manton
    • A monumental work (1658).
    • 376 pages on 25 verses!
  3. Jude in Everyman’s Bible Commentary by S. Maxwell Coder
    • Brief but satisfying.

REVELATION

  1. The Apocalypse, A Series of Special Lectures on the Revelation of Jesus Christ by J.A. Seiss
    • One of the first popular premillennial futurist commentaries on Revelation, and perhaps the most influential.
    • By a well-known Lutheran writer of the past century.
    • “...the most famous expository work on Revelation in our languages … sane, suggestive, reverent … dependable” — Wilbur M. Smith
  2. Lectures on the Apocalypse by R. Govett
    • Originally four volumes, now abridged to two.
    • Was born in 1813 and died in 1901. A fellow of Worcester College, Oxford.
    • Highly recommended by Watchman Nee.
    • “Mr. Govett wrote a hundred years before his time, and the day will come when his works will be treasured as sifted gold” — C.H.Spurgeon
    • “One of profoundest volumes that I know of is the work of Robert Govett.”
    • “Govett summarizes what most commentators have attempted to say.”
    • “My own opinion is that he brings to his interpretation a more thorough knowledge of the Scriptures in their bearing on the last book of the Bible than any other writer of his generation.” — Wilbur Smith
    • "In all my life I have discovered no other author so exactly aware of what God has said; and who is able to make it clear in plain and simple language.” — David M. Panton
    • “Readers able to profit by the full discussions of the learned will naturally turn to such works as those by Alford, Delitzsch, GOVETT, William Kelly or Westcott.” — G. H. Lang
    • “By far, Govett was the best systematic theologian” — R.E.D.Clarke, Ph.D. Cambridge Univ. (Emeritus)
  3. Revelation in the Anglican Commentary by William Lee
    • Especially valuable because the author gives comprehensive surveys of the different interpretations of a disputed passage.
  4. The Great Unveiling by W. G. Scroggie
    • A study guide that presents the different schools of interpretation.
    • Good treatment of Christological approach and of symbolism.
  5. Come Lord Jesus by W. Nee
    • Valuable because Nee presents the different schools of interpretation and then concludes with what he considers to be the thing that is closer to the truth.
  6. The Revelation of Jesus Christ by G. H. Lang
    • The author was born 1874 and died 1958.
    • Perhaps one of the greatest teachers of his time.
    • The author once said, “No man should write a book until he is 40. He needs to prove his theories in practice before publishing.”
  7. The Great Prophecies by G.H. Pember
    • Four volume series.
    • One name (among two or three) that dominated prophetic study in the Victorian age.
    • A writer foremost in scholarship, in expository insight, in literary clarity.
    • The value of this present volume becomes correspondingly greater as it gives to the present generation a summary of one who knew his Bible. — D.M. Panton
    • “He was preeminently a teacher of teachers, and one of the best exponents of prophetic Scripture during his period” — G. H. Lang
    • “One of the most valuable expositions of prophecy ever published” — Prophetic News
    • “This is a book of distinct and conspicuous mark on the exhaustless theme of Scripture Prophecy” — United Presbyterian Magazine
  8. Lectures on the Book of Revelation by William Kelly
    • A premillennial approach based on a literal interpretation of the text.
    • Regarded as an important contribution.
  9. Commentary on the Revelation of John by G.E. Ladd
    • Post-tribulational.
    • An excellent study.
    • "A solid and useful commentary on the Apocalypse" — Merril C. Tenney
    • "A balanced, scholarly, devout treatment of this very difficult book of Scripture." — Harold Ockenga, President Gordon College
  10. The Revelation of Jesus Christ by J. F. Walvoord
    • Pre-tribulationist, and dispensational.
    • An excellent study, thoroughly abreast of the latest scholarly research.
    • The author has specialized in the field of biblical eschatology for more than fifty years.
    • “One of the best, most reasonable and most restrained of its kind.” — Watchman Examiner
  11. Exposition of the Revelation of Jesus Christ by Walter Scott
    • A classic treatment of the English text.
    • Strongly typological.
  12. Annotations on the Revelation of St. John the Divine in the Lutheran Commentary series by R. F. Weidner
    • Regarded as exceptionally valuable.
  13. The Apocalypse of St. John by Barclay Swete
    • Thorough treatment of content of Revelation.
    • On the Greek text of Revelation, there is perhaps nothing equal to it.
    • The author’s knowledge of the meaning of the Greek words is unparalleled among interpreters of his day.
  14. The Doctrine of the Apocalypse by Hermann Gebhardt
    • A work too rarely seen today.
  15. The Prophecies of Daniel and the Revelations of St. John, Viewed in Their Mutual Relation by Carl August Auberlen
    • A distinguished Swiss theologian.
    • “one of the truly great works on prophecy of the 19th century” — Wilbur Smith
  16. Interpreting Revelation by Merrill C. Tenney
    • An introduction to the various methods of interpretation.
    • “Dr. Tenney has here succeeded again in condensing a great deal of learning into a relatively small compass. He is able to present difficult and complicated issues in a simple and intelligent manner, and with all fairness to his predecessors to chart his own original approach to the problem.” — Otto A. Piper
  17. Horae Apocalyptictae by E.B. Elliott
    • A monumental work, four volumes.
    • One of the best examples of the historicist school.
    • Postmillennial.
    • Contains a wealth of material on the history of interpretation.
  18. Lectures on the Revelation by H.A. Ironside
    • Futurist view presented in popular lecture style.
  19. The Revelation of St. John by Abraham Kuyper
    • Some very good suggestions.
  20. The Revelation of John by J.P. Lange
    • Exhaustive in detail.
  21. Of Things Which Soon Must Come To Pass by Philip Mauro
    • Attempted fusion of different views.
    • General abandonment of futurist position, with spiritualized millennium.
  22. Studies in the Revelation of St. John by Albertus Pieters
    • Amillennial.
    • Good classified bibliography.
  23. Leading Thoughts on the Apocalypse by Robert Govett
    • Topical study.
  24. Christ, Our Revelation by Mme. Guyon
    • As a well-known mystic, the author sees the book of Revelation as a record of the persecution of Christians being persecuted for their walk in the deeper Christian life.
  25. Commentary on Revelation by E. W. Bullinger
    • This is a very different commentary.
    • The author holds to the view that the church has been raptured before the Book of Revelation opens.
  26. The Revelation in The Greek Testament by Henry Alford
    • A classic and still valuable interpretation from the historical point of view.
  27. The Revelation in The New Bible Commentary by G. R. Beasley-Murray
    • A conservative scholar who combines the presterist and the futurist viewpoint.
  28. The Apocalypse of John by Isbon T. Beckwith
    • One of the best commentaries in English.
    • Contains outstanding introductory studies on the interpretation of the Revelation, etc., followed by over 400 pages of commentary.
    • A work of impeccable scholarship.
    • Amillennial.
  29. The Revelation of St. John The Define by G.B. Caird
    • The author understands much of the symbolism.
    • Very readable.
  30. More Than Conquerors by W. Hendriksen
    • Symbolical interpretation.
    • Perhaps the best amillennial treatment.
  31. Revelation by Leon Morris
    • An outstanding conservative scholar who combines the presterist and futurist view.
  32. Worthy is the Lamb by Ray Summers
    • A conservative scholar interprets chapters 1:1-20:10 from the pretherist viewpoint and only 20:11-22:5 as eschatology.
  33. The Book of the Revelation: Outlined Studies by Lehman Strauss
    • Both Wilbur M. Smith and Warren W. Wiersbe thought very highly of this work.
  34. The Book of the Revelation in New Commentary on the New Testament by Robert H. Mounce
    • Scholarly.
  35. Understanding Revelation by Gary G. Cohen
    • Exceedingly useful.
  36. Behold He Cometh! An Exposition of the Book of Revelation by Herman Hoeksema
    • An amillennial exposition.
  37. A Revelation of Jesus Christ by Jacob Brubaker Smith
    • Provides rich source material.
  38. Prophecy’s Last Word by Frederick A. Tatford
    • A valuable reference work.
    • Premillennial.
